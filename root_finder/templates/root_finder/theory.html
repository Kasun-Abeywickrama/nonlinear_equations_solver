Theoretical Background

Introduction

Numerical methods for solving nonlinear equations are essential tools in computational mathematics and engineering. These methods provide iterative approaches to find roots (zeros) of functions where analytical solutions are difficult or impossible to obtain.

A nonlinear equation has the form $f(x) = 0$, where $f$ is a nonlinear function. The goal is to find values of $x$ (called roots) that satisfy this equation. The three methods implemented here represent different approaches with varying convergence properties and computational requirements.

1. Bisection Method

Theory

The bisection method is based on the Intermediate Value Theorem. If a continuous function $f(x)$ changes sign over an interval $[a,b]$, then there exists at least one root in that interval.

Algorithm Steps:

  1. Verify that $f(a) \cdot f(b) < 0$ (root is bracketed)
  2. Calculate midpoint: $c = \frac{a + b}{2}$
  3. Evaluate $f(c)$
  4. If $f(a) \cdot f(c) < 0$, set $b = c$; otherwise set $a = c$
  5. Repeat until convergence

Convergence Analysis

Convergence Rate: Linear (order 1)

Error Bound: After $n$ iterations: $|e_n| \leq \frac{|b_0 - a_0|}{2^n}$

The error decreases by a factor of 2 in each iteration, guaranteeing convergence but at a relatively slow rate.

Advantages

  • Always converges if root is bracketed
  • Simple to implement and understand
  • Robust and reliable
  • Does not require derivative

Disadvantages

  • Slow convergence (linear)
  • Requires initial bracketing interval
  • Cannot find complex roots
  • May miss multiple roots in interval

2. Newton-Raphson Method

Theory

The Newton-Raphson method uses linear approximation (tangent line) at each iteration to estimate the root location. The method is derived from Taylor series expansion of $f(x)$ around the current approximation.

Iteration Formula:

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$

where $f'(x_n)$ is the derivative of $f$ evaluated at $x_n$

Geometric Interpretation:

At each iteration, we find where the tangent line to $f(x)$ at point $(x_n, f(x_n))$ intersects the x-axis. This intersection point becomes the next approximation $x_{n+1}$.

Convergence Analysis

Convergence Rate: Quadratic (order 2) near simple roots

Convergence Condition: $|f'(x)| > 0$ and $x_0$ sufficiently close to root

Error Relation: $|e_{n+1}| \approx C|e_n|^2$ where $C = \frac{|f''(r)|}{2|f'(r)|}$

Conditions for Convergence

  • $f'(x) \neq 0$ near the root (non-zero derivative)
  • Initial guess $x_0$ must be sufficiently close to the root
  • Function should be smooth (twice differentiable)

Advantages

  • Quadratic convergence (very fast)
  • Only needs one initial guess
  • High accuracy with few iterations
  • Well-established mathematical foundation

Disadvantages

  • Requires derivative calculation
  • May diverge with poor initial guess
  • Fails when $f'(x) = 0$
  • Sensitive to multiple roots

3. Secant Method

Theory

The secant method approximates the derivative in Newton's method using finite differences. Instead of using the analytical derivative, it uses a secant line through two previous points to estimate the slope.

Iteration Formula:

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n) \cdot \frac{x_n - x_{n-1}}{f(x_n) - f(x_{n-1})}$$

Requires two initial guesses: $x_0$ and $x_1$

Derivative Approximation:

$$f'(x_n) \approx \frac{f(x_n) - f(x_{n-1})}{x_n - x_{n-1}}$$

Convergence Analysis

Convergence Rate: Superlinear (order ≈ 1.618, the golden ratio φ)

Error Relation: $|e_{n+1}| \approx C|e_n|^{1.618}$

Convergence is faster than bisection but slower than Newton-Raphson, making it a good compromise when derivatives are difficult to compute.

Comparison with Newton-Raphson

  • Similarity: Both use linear approximation concepts
  • Key Difference: Secant uses numerical derivative approximation
  • Trade-off: Slower convergence but no derivative needed
  • Efficiency: Often more practical for complex functions

Advantages

  • No derivative calculation required
  • Superlinear convergence
  • Good balance of speed and robustness
  • Suitable for complex functions

Disadvantages

  • Requires two initial guesses
  • May fail if $f(x_n) \approx f(x_{n-1})$
  • Slower than Newton-Raphson
  • Less predictable than bisection

Comparative Analysis

Aspect Bisection Newton-Raphson Secant
Convergence Rate Linear (1) Quadratic (2) Superlinear (~1.618)
Initial Requirements Bracketing interval One guess + derivative Two guesses
Reliability Always converges May diverge Generally reliable
Function Evaluations 1 per iteration 2 per iteration (f + f') 1 per iteration
Best Use Case When reliability is critical When speed is important Balance of speed & simplicity

Practical Considerations

Choosing the Right Method

  • Bisection: When you need guaranteed convergence and have a bracketing interval
  • Newton-Raphson: When derivative is available and high accuracy is needed quickly
  • Secant: When derivative is unknown/expensive and moderate speed is acceptable

Common Pitfalls

  • Poor initial guesses can cause divergence
  • Functions with multiple roots require careful analysis
  • Numerical precision limits affect convergence criteria
  • Oscillatory or discontinuous functions may cause issues

Mathematical Foundations

Key Theorems

Intermediate Value Theorem: If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $f(a) \cdot f(b) < 0$, then there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f(c) = 0$.
Newton's Method Convergence: If $f$ is twice continuously differentiable and $f'(r) \neq 0$ at root $r$, then Newton's method converges quadratically for sufficiently close initial guesses.
Fixed Point Theorem: Root-finding can be reformulated as finding fixed points of $g(x) = x - \frac{f(x)}{f'(x)}$ (Newton) or similar transformations.
